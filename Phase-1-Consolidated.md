## 1. Pick a Niche (and Resist Going Wide)

**Core question:** Who, specifically, are you for? What industry + what problem + what type of person?

**Why it's a blocker:** "I help businesses use AI" is not a niche. Specialized consultants command 30–40% fee premiums over generalists. Clients increasingly search for domain-specific expertise. The tighter the niche, the easier everything else gets — pricing, messaging, finding clients.

**What the research says:**
- Your niche isn't what you do — it's who you serve and what specific problem you solve for them
- Pick based on where you have existing knowledge *and* where real pain and budget exist
- You don't need to be an industry expert — you just need to know more about AI than they do
- Start with the smallest defensible segment where you can be 10x better than alternatives, then expand
- Examples: AI automation for real estate agencies, AI content workflows for e-commerce brands, AI customer service for dental practices

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Niche, Nail, Scale** | Pick one narrow niche → win consistently there → only expand once you've mastered it |
| **3-Part ICP** | Who (firmographics) + When (the trigger that makes them need you now) + Why Now (the macro trend creating urgency) |
| **Jobs to Be Done** (Clayton Christensen) | What "job" is your client hiring you to do? What were they doing before you existed? |
| **Beachhead Market Rule** | Dominate the smallest viable segment first; expansion comes after proof of dominance |

## 2. Positioning & Messaging — What Do You Actually Say?

**Core question:** What problem do you solve, in one sentence, without using the word "AI"?

**Why it's a blocker:** 67% of software company websites now mention AI — the word has become noise. Most AI business owners can describe what they do technically but can't explain the business outcome in plain language. Clients buy outcomes, not technology.

**What the research says:**
- Lead with outcomes, not technology: "I help real estate agents reclaim 10 hours a week" outperforms "I build AI automation solutions"
- One-sentence offer formula: *"I help [specific client] [achieve specific outcome] without [the thing they're afraid of]."*
- Your differentiator doesn't have to be your technology — it can be your industry focus, your process, your speed, your teaching style, or your lived experience
- Test it: say it to 5 people in your target market; if 3 say "wait, how does that work?" — you've got it

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Obviously Awesome** (April Dunford) | 5-part positioning: competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value those deliver → target who cares most → market frame of reference |
| **StoryBrand** (Donald Miller) | The client is the hero; you are the guide. Lead with their problem, not your credentials. |
| **"Unlike X, We Y" contrast messaging** | Lead with what makes you different by naming what you're *not*, then what you are |

## 3. Validate Before You Build

**Core question:** Will anyone actually pay for this — and can you find 3 real signals *before* you build anything?

**Why it's a blocker:** Most people skip this and spend weeks building something nobody wants to pay for. The signal to look for isn't "is this a good idea?" — it's "has someone already complained about this problem and tried to pay someone to fix it?"

**What the research says:**
- Apply the 3-filter test before investing time in any idea: Does the problem happen at least weekly? Does it cost the business $50K+/year? Is there a budget owner who can approve spending within 30 days?
- Have 10 real conversations with potential clients before building anything — if at least 7 describe the same pain and signal willingness to pay, you have something real
- "Validate with currency" — a polite "sounds great!" is not validation; someone asking for an invoice is
- The fastest real-world test: describe the thing before it exists and see if people inquire or sign up

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **The Mom Test** (Rob Fitzpatrick) | Ask about their life and past behavior, not their opinion of your idea — gets past polite lies |
| **Lean Startup Build-Measure-Learn** (Eric Ries) | Form hypothesis → smallest possible test → measure real signal → repeat |
| **2-20-200 Rule** | 2 hrs desk research → 20 hrs talking to potential customers → only 200 hrs building *after* strong signals |
| **Fake Door / Smoke Test** | Create a landing page or offer describing the thing before it exists — real inquiries = real demand |

## 4. Find and Land Your First Client

**Core question:** Who in your existing network has the problem you solve — and how do you have that conversation?

**Why it's a blocker:** Most people either wait for clients to come to them (they don't) or jump straight to cold outreach (premature). Your first client almost always comes from your existing network.

**What the research says:**
- Start warm: former coworkers, managers, classmates, business contacts — tell them what you're doing and ask if they know anyone who could use your help
- LinkedIn: search for people matching your ICP, connect with a personalized note (not a pitch), add value before asking for anything
- Lead with a low-risk entry offer (audit or diagnostic, $1,500–$2,500) — easier "yes" for them, proof of value for you
- Offer first engagement at a reduced rate in exchange for a testimonial and the right to use them as a case study
- Warm referrals convert at dramatically higher rates than any other channel

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **The Referral Map** | Write 15–20 names from memory → assign comfort level → warm them up → ask for intros |
| **Hormozi First 5 Clients** (Alex Hormozi) | Export contacts → pick platform you have most → send personalized messages → ask for referrals → make an easy offer |
| **Show, Don't Tell Demo** | Build something small applied to their business and show it — turns a pitch into proof |

## 5. Pricing — How Much, How Do You Charge, and for What?

**Core question:** What model fits your service, and how do you set a price that doesn't make you cringe?

**Why it's a blocker:** Underpricing is the most common mistake. Half of potential clients report budgets under $2,000 — those aren't your clients. Pricing too low signals low confidence and attracts difficult clients.

**What the research says — 2025–2026 market rates:**
- Hourly: $100–$150 (junior) / $150–$300 (mid) / $300–$500+ (expert)
- Day rates: $600–$1,200/day
- Project-based: $10K–$40K (small/pilot) / $40K–$150K (medium, multi-month)
- Retainers: $2K–$5K/month (5–10 hrs) / $5K–$15K/month (10–25 hrs)
- Productized services: most scalable; 44% of consultants now offer one
- Value-based pricing (10–40% of the client's cost savings or revenue gain) is the fastest-growing model

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Value-Based Pricing** (Blair Enns / Jonathan Stark) | Price based on client ROI, not your hours — ask what solving this is *worth* to them |
| **3-Tier Anchoring (Good/Better/Best)** | Offer three packages; most clients land in the middle; anchors expectations |
| **Productized Service Model** | Fixed scope + fixed price + repeatable delivery = a service that behaves like a product |
| **50/50 Payment Rule** | 50% upfront before work starts, 50% on delivery — filters non-serious clients, protects your time |

## 6. Set Up Your Business Structure

**Core question:** Sole proprietor or LLC — and what do you actually need to do to register?

**Why it's a blocker:** Many enterprise clients won't engage sole proprietors — their procurement departments require a formal business entity. Without a business bank account, you can't separate personal and business finances, which creates tax headaches and looks unprofessional.

**What the research says:**
- Single-member LLC is the most common first step for AI consultants: $100–$500 to form + $150–$800/year in state fees
- Creates a legal firewall between personal finances and business, adds credibility when signing contracts
- Get an EIN (free, 10 minutes at IRS.gov) — required for business bank accounts and tax filing
- You can start invoicing as a sole proprietor under your own name while the LLC is being set up — don't let structure setup delay your first conversation

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **SBA Business Structure Decision Tree** | Sole Proprietor → LLC → S-Corp based on income level and liability needs |
| **Solo Legal Setup Checklist** | Form LLC → Get EIN → Open separate business bank account → Get a contract template |

## 7. Run a Proper Discovery Process

**Core question:** What questions do you ask before you send a proposal?

**Why it's a blocker:** Most scope creep, undercharging, and unhappy clients trace back to a bad or missing discovery conversation. Proposals written without real discovery almost always miss the mark.

**What the research says:**
- Before any proposal, you need to understand: What's the actual problem? What does success look like in 90 days? What's already been tried? What's out of scope?
- Ask about emotions, not just logistics — clients' fear of change and internal politics are often the real blockers
- Co-creating the roadmap with the client (not for them) eliminates scope creep because they defined the scope themselves
- The 5 core scoping questions answer everything you need to write any proposal

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **CLEAR Discovery** | Capture goal → Learn current approach → Examine emotions → Acknowledge → Reveal additional context |
| **5 Core Scoping Questions** | What problem are you solving? What does success look like? What's your deadline? What have you tried? What's out of scope? |
| **Co-Created Roadmap** | Build the project plan *with* the client during kickoff, not for them afterward |

## 8. Build Your Delivery System

**Core question:** What's your repeatable process from "yes, I'll work with you" to "here's your final deliverable"?

**Why it's a blocker:** Over 50% of AI consulting projects fail due to scope creep and unclear priorities (McKinsey 2025). The biggest time drain for new AI business owners is recreating the same process for every client. Templates built once save hours every engagement.

**What the research says:**
- Create a client onboarding checklist, a SOW template, and a status update format before you need them
- Define scope in writing before any work starts — what's included, what's not, what done looks like
- Add a change request clause to every contract so scope creep has a process instead of a fight
- Weekly 3-sentence status updates (what happened / what's next / any blockers) are standard — clients who feel informed don't panic

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Client Journey Map** | Pre-onboarding → Kickoff → Milestone check-ins → Final delivery → Offboarding/handoff |
| **Template Library** | Reusable docs for proposals, SOWs, status updates, final delivery, and offboarding |
| **Productized Delivery Playbook** | Step-by-step internal doc for how you deliver your signature service — foundation for delegation |

## 9. Contracts & Getting Paid

**Core question:** How do you get paid reliably, without a lawyer on retainer?

**Why it's a blocker:** This is the #1 thing new AI business owners report being caught off guard by — scope creep, late payments, IP ownership of AI outputs, and what to do when someone doesn't pay. Most skip contracts entirely until they get burned.

**What the research says:**
- Use a simple Statement of Work for every project — cover scope, what's NOT included, timeline, and payment terms
- Net 15 or Net 30 payment terms are standard; require 25–50% deposit upfront on project work
- Follow up on overdue invoices on day 1 after the due date — this is professional, not rude
- IP ownership of AI-generated work is increasingly a contract issue — address it explicitly
- Set aside 25–30% of every payment for taxes immediately; pay quarterly estimated taxes

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Simple SOW Template** | What you'll do / what you won't / timeline / payment terms / what client provides — one page |
| **Net 15 / Net 30 Terms** | Invoice due within 15 or 30 days; late fee clause included |
| **IP & AI Output Clause** | Specifies who owns the AI-generated work product — increasingly critical, often missed |

## 10. Set Up Your Operations Stack

**Core question:** What's the minimum toolset to run your business professionally?

**Why it's a blocker:** Using the wrong tools (or no tools) creates invisible friction — missed follow-ups, lost invoices, scattered client files. The danger zone is paying for tools you don't use, or over-engineering before you have clients.

**What the research says:**
- Most lean AI businesses run on under $150/month in tools
- Start with exactly 4 tools; don't add more until you have 3+ clients
- The integration that matters most: connect your scheduling tool to your calendar — automates confirmations and eliminates no-shows

**Minimum Viable Stack:**
| Function | Tool | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Invoicing | Wave | Free |
| Scheduling | Calendly | Free tier |
| Project tracking | Notion or ClickUp | Free tier |
| Contracts | HelloSign or DocuSign | ~$15/mo |

**Add at 3+ clients:** HubSpot (free CRM), QuickBooks Online (~$30/mo)

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Minimum Viable Stack** | Claude/ChatGPT + Calendly + HelloSign + Stripe + Notion — under $150/month total |
| **Tool Audit Rule** | If you haven't used a tool in 30 days, cancel it |
| **The "Do I Need This?" Test** | Does NOT having this tool cause me to lose money, miss follow-ups, or make mistakes with clients? If no — don't add it. |

## 11. Promote Yourself Consistently (Not Occasionally)

**Core question:** How do you talk about your work without feeling like you're bragging?

**Why it's a blocker:** This is the #1 behavioral gap. Most people do great work and tell nobody — or only promote when business is slow, by which point it's too late. Authenticity and teaching consistently outperform polished brand content.

**What the research says:**
- Building in public (showing your process, including the messy parts) builds more trust than polished case studies
- 80/20 rule: use AI to produce volume (80% of posts), spend your energy on the 20% that shows real expertise — original observations, hard-won lessons
- One platform, consistently: pick LinkedIn, Twitter/X, or a newsletter and show up 3–5x per week for 90 days before evaluating results
- Turn every client win into content: one thing → five pieces (post, carousel, newsletter, video, framework)
- Don't wait until you have a polished offering — "here's what I'm discovering about [niche]" is promotable content from day one

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Build in Public** | Share the process, not just the outcome — the messy middle, the decisions, the lessons |
| **1-to-5 Repurposing** | One piece of real work → LinkedIn post → carousel → newsletter section → 60-sec video → reusable framework |
| **StoryBrand for Social** | Every post: here's a problem your audience has → here's what I learned → here's what you can do |
| **$1.80 Strategy** (Gary Vee) | Comment meaningfully on 9 posts/day in your niche, leaving your genuine "2 cents" — builds visibility before you ask for anything |

## 12. Build Credibility Before You Have Clients

**Core question:** How do you build social proof when you're starting from zero?

**Why it's a blocker:** The chicken-and-egg problem — you need case studies to get clients, but you need clients to get case studies. This stops people before they start.

**What the research says:**
- Offer 1–2 free or reduced-price engagements specifically in exchange for a detailed testimonial and permission to write a case study
- Building in public (sharing your process, not just outcomes) builds trust faster than polished case studies
- A workshop, webinar, or live demo of your service creates credibility before you have paying clients
- The "show don't tell" demo — build something small applied to *their* business and show it — converts dramatically better than describing what you do

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **The Free Work Ladder** | 1–2 free/discounted engagements → in exchange for a testimonial + case study rights |
| **Content Credibility Stack** | Certifications + testimonials + a workshop + documented results, stacked over time |
| **Build in Public** | Share the process of building your business — what you're learning, what's working, what isn't |
| **Show, Don't Tell Demo** | Build a small demo of your service applied to *their* business and show it before pitching |

## 13. Grow Beyond the First Client

**Core question:** How do you turn one client into three?

**Why it's a blocker:** Growth is not automatic. Most consultants stay stuck at 2–3 clients because they don't build the referral and case study systems. Most AI businesses that survive past 6 months do so on repeat clients and referrals — not a constant stream of new leads.

**What the research says:**
- Ask for a referral at the moment of highest client satisfaction — right after they see the first real result, not at project close
- Write a case study for every completed project: problem → approach → result (with numbers). Three sentences minimum
- Design natural upsell paths before the current project ends — have the next offer ready to plant during the final debrief
- Community visibility (building in public with WDAI) generates the kind of organic referrals that cold outreach never produces

**Named frameworks:**
| Framework | What it is |
|---|---|
| **The Direct Referral Ask** | At peak happiness: "Who else do you know who has this problem? Would you be comfortable introducing me?" |
| **Case Study Template** | Problem → What we did → Result (quantified) → Client quote |
| **Upsell Ladder** | Audit → Project → Retainer → Expanded scope — each engagement seeds the next |

## Track-Specific Research Notes

### Consulting track
- First client almost always comes from existing network, not cold outreach
- Scope creep is the #1 delivery risk — manage contracts carefully from day one
- Specialization commands 30–40% higher fees than generalism
- Credibility is built through visible thinking (writing, teaching) more than credentials

### Workflows / Automation track
- Cold outreach using the automation tools you sell is the most common client acquisition channel
- Demo videos showing real automation workflows convert better than any other marketing
- Pricing resistance is high — ROI calculators and graduated service tiers overcome it
- Target businesses already experiencing growth and struggling to scale — they're most receptive

### Apps track
- Validation is even more important here — the build cost is much higher
- A non-technical founder needs a technical co-founder or contractor before anything else
- MVP scoping discipline is critical — most app projects fail from over-scoping
- The co-founder / contractor question must be answered *before* discovery — a non-technical founder who validates an idea and finds a niche still can't move forward without knowing whether they can build it alone or need a technical partner. This is a blocker that surfaces earlier than on any other track.
- Product-market fit is not a milestone — it's a loop. Ship something small → get it in front of real users → measure retention and return behavior → learn → ship again. The signal you're looking for: users come back without being prompted, and would be "very disappointed" if the product went away (Sean Ellis / Superhuman test). Expect to run 3–5 loops before the product clicks.

## The Overlooked Things (What Most Guides Skip)

- **Getting paid on time is a skill.** Invoice the moment a milestone is hit. Follow up on day 1 after a due date. This feels uncomfortable and is completely normal and expected in professional services.
- **The first 6–12 months consume more internal time than client time.** Building your templates, onboarding, pricing, and systems takes longer than expected.
- **Community is a competitive advantage.** In a commoditizing market, people buy from people they know and trust. WDAI is itself a moat — lean into it.
- **~25% of consulting skills will become obsolete by mid-2026.** Continuous learning isn't optional — it's the business model.
- **IP ownership of AI outputs is an emerging contract issue.** Address it explicitly in every agreement now, before it becomes a dispute.
