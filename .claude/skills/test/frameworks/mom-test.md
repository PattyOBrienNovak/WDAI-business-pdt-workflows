# Framework: The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

**Use when:** the user hasn't talked to real people yet, or their only evidence is a hunch/opinion ("I think people would want this"). The Mom Test exists to get past polite lies — even your mom will tell you your idea is great if you ask her opinion of it. Ask about their life and past behavior instead.

**The idea (teach it in one line):** Don't ask people what they think of your idea — ask about their life, their past behavior, and what they've already tried. Opinions about hypothetical futures are worthless; stories about specific past moments are gold.

**Guardrail specific to this framework:** if the user starts describing their *idea* or *solution* mid-flow, redirect: "Let's stay on their life for now — tell me about a specific time this actually happened to them, not what they'd think of a fix."

## Question flow

Ask one at a time. After each answer, reflect it back in one line before asking the next.

### Q1 — Whose life are we talking about?
> Picture the specific person who might have this problem. Tell me about them — their role, what their day actually looks like.

### Q2 — A specific past moment
> Tell me about the last time this problem actually came up for them — a real, specific moment, not a general feeling. What were they doing right before it happened?

### Q3 — What they actually did about it
> What did they actually do about it — not what they should have done, what they *did* do? Did they try to fix it, pay for something, ask someone, or just live with it? Be specific.

### Q4 — What it's costing them
> What has this actually cost them so far — in time, money, missed opportunities, or stress? Use real numbers or examples if you have them, not estimates.

### Q5 — What "solved" would look like, grounded in their reality
> Based on everything they've actually done and tried — not a hypothetical fix — what would have to be true for this to feel handled? What would change in their day?

After Q5, hand back to the main skill for synthesis.
