## How a COO Operates — Explained to a Beginner Founder

**The one-line version:** If the CEO is the **architect** (decides what to build and why), the COO is the **builder** (makes sure the structure actually stands up). The CEO sets the vision; the COO turns it into something that runs day after day without falling over.

### The four things every COO really does

Strip away the corporate language and every COO — from a Fortune 500 to a solo consultant — does four things:

1. **Builds the machine.** Sets up the infrastructure the business runs on — legal entity, bank accounts, tools, contracts. The plumbing.
2. **Runs the work reliably.** Makes sure that when a client says yes, the work gets scoped, delivered, and closed out the same way every time — not reinvented from scratch each engagement.
3. **Makes sure money moves correctly.** Contracts get signed, invoices go out, payments come in, taxes get set aside. The financial discipline most founders avoid.
4. **Removes friction.** Finds the bottleneck that's slowing everything down and fixes it. Then finds the next one.

### The mindset shift that defines the role

The single most important idea in small-business operations comes from Michael Gerber's *The E-Myth Revisited*:

> **Work ON your business, not IN it.**

Gerber's insight is brutal and exactly right for this audience: most people who start a business are *technicians* (great at the actual work — the AI building, the consulting) who wrongly assume that being good at the work means they can run the business. They can't, because doing the work and building the system that does the work are completely different jobs.

The COO is the role that builds **systems** — so the business can run consistently even when the founder is busy, tired, or eventually wants to hand work off. Gerber calls this the "franchise prototype" mindset: build it as if you'll need to hand the instructions to someone else, even if that someone is just future-you.

**For a solo WDAI founder, this is the liberating reframe:** you don't have to be heroic. You build the system once, then the system carries the load.

### Why the COO is the "unsexy but make-or-break" role

From your own Phase 1 research: **over 50% of AI consulting projects fail due to scope creep and unclear priorities** (McKinsey 2025). That's not a sales problem or a product problem — it's an *operations* problem. The COO is the role that prevents the failure mode that kills the most AI businesses.

It's also where the biggest hidden time drain lives: Phase 1 notes that recreating the same process for every client is the #1 time sink for new AI business owners. The COO's job is to make that "once, then reuse forever."

## The Recommended Evergreen Frameworks

### 1. The E-Myth — Work ON Your Business, Not IN It ★ (The systemizing mindset)

**Source:** Michael Gerber, *The E-Myth Revisited* (1995). The single most influential small-business operations book ever written. Foundational reading for EOS/Traction.

**What it is:** Most small businesses are built by *technicians* who assume being good at the work means they can run the business. Gerber's fix: think like a franchise. Build every part of your business as a documented, repeatable system you could hand to someone else. Three roles live in every founder — the Entrepreneur (vision), the Manager (order/systems), the Technician (the doer). The COO is the Manager.

**Why it fits:** This is THE reframe for WDAI members. They are technicians (deep expertise in their field + AI). The E-Myth gives them permission and a method to stop being heroic and start building systems. It's the philosophical spine of the whole COO role.

**How it powers a command:** Powers `/coo-systemize` (the capstone/advanced command) and informs the "build it as a reusable template" approach across every COO command.

---

### 2. Theory of Constraints — Find the One Bottleneck ★ (Where to focus)

**Source:** Eliyahu Goldratt, *The Goal* (1984). A business novel that's sold over 7 million copies; still taught in operations courses worldwide.

**What it is:** Every system has exactly one bottleneck that limits its total output. Optimizing anything *other* than the bottleneck is wasted effort. The method: (1) Identify the constraint, (2) Exploit it (get maximum from it), (3) Subordinate everything else to it, (4) Elevate it (add capacity), (5) Repeat — find the next constraint.

**Why it fits:** Solo founders waste enormous energy optimizing the wrong thing — perfecting their logo while their invoicing leaks cash. Theory of Constraints forces the question: *what is the ONE thing slowing everything down right now?* For most early founders, the bottleneck is sales conversations or delivery time — rarely the thing they're fiddling with.

**How it powers a command:** Powers `/coo-systemize` — diagnoses the current bottleneck before suggesting which system to build next.

---

### 3. PDCA / The Deming Cycle — Continuous Improvement (How delivery gets better)

**Source:** W. Edwards Deming (popularized post-WWII; the engine behind the Japanese quality revolution). Plan → Do → Check → Act.

**What it is:** A loop for improving any repeatable process. **Plan** the approach → **Do** it on a real engagement → **Check** what worked and what didn't → **Act** to update the system. Then run the loop again. Each cycle makes the delivery system a little better.

**Why it fits:** Patty's Client Project Lifecycle is the process; PDCA is how it *improves* over time. After each client, the founder asks "what should change in my system?" instead of repeating the same friction forever.

**How it powers a command:** Built into `/coo-deliver` (the post-engagement debrief) and `/coo-systemize` (the improvement loop).

---

### 4. The Checklist Manifesto — Reliability Under Complexity (How to deliver without dropping balls)

**Source:** Atul Gawande, *The Checklist Manifesto* (2009). Showed that simple checklists dramatically cut errors in surgery, aviation, and construction.

**What it is:** When work is complex and the cost of forgetting a step is high, a simple checklist outperforms expertise and memory. Not because people are dumb — because complexity exceeds what anyone can reliably hold in their head.

**Why it fits:** This is the direct antidote to the 50%-of-projects-fail-from-scope-creep stat. A client onboarding checklist, a pre-delivery checklist, a project-close checklist — these turn "I hope I remembered everything" into "I know I did." Perfect for a solo founder juggling multiple clients.

**How it powers a command:** Powers the artifact outputs of `/coo-deliver` — every phase of the Client Project Lifecycle becomes a literal checklist.

---

### 5. EOS / Traction — The Operating System (Advanced / later-stage)

**Source:** Gino Wickman, *Traction* (2011). The "Entrepreneurial Operating System" — a plug-and-play version of Gerber's philosophy.

**What it is:** A complete operating system for running a business on six components (Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, Traction). Includes weekly meeting rhythms, scorecards, and role clarity.

**Why it fits:** Mostly relevant *once the founder adds a first team member or contractor.* Before that, it's overkill. Worth naming as the "graduation" framework so members know where the COO role evolves to.

**How it powers a command:** Reference only for now — informs `/coo-systemize` and a future "first hire" trigger. Not a launch command.

## What's Intentionally Left Out (And Why)

| Framework | Why we're skipping it |
|---|---|
| **Six Sigma / Lean Manufacturing** | Built for high-volume manufacturing. Massive overkill for a solo services business. |
| **RACI Matrix** | Useful for teams to assign responsibility — but a solo founder is R, A, C, and I. Add when they hire. |
| **Full EOS implementation** | Named as the "graduation" framework above, but the full system is a team tool, not a solo-founder tool. |
| **ITIL / formal service management** | Enterprise IT ops. Irrelevant at this stage. |
| **OKRs for operations** | OKRs already live in the CEO role. No need to duplicate. |

### The contracts split — protection vs. collection

`/coo-contracts` and `/coo-getpaid` were deliberately split because they solve two different anxieties at two different moments:

- **`/coo-contracts`** is about **risk** — the document you sign once that protects you if things go wrong. The big AI-specific issue (IP ownership of AI-generated outputs) lives here. A "set it up right" move, *before* work starts.
- **`/coo-getpaid`** is about **cashflow** — the recurring discipline of actually collecting. Phase 1's "Overlooked Things" explicitly says *"Getting paid on time is a skill."* The overdue scripts and tax rhythm are recurring behaviors, not one-time paperwork. It earns its own command because founders avoid it.

A beginner thinks "contract" and "getting paid" as separate worries — so the commands match that mental model.

## How to Actually Run Them — The Step-by-Step Sequence

Unlike CRO (which had a draft→refine loop), the COO sequence is mostly linear, with a one-time setup block and a repeating per-client loop:

```
ONE-TIME SETUP (do once, early — before your first client)
   /coo-setup     →  legal entity, bank account, business email
   /coo-stack     →  the 4 starter tools
   /coo-contracts →  protective agreements ready to sign

PER-CLIENT LOOP (repeat every engagement)
   /coo-scope     →  scope it (after CRO closes the deal)
   /coo-deliver   →  deliver it reliably (kickoff → milestones → close)
   /coo-getpaid   →  invoice, collect, set aside taxes

ADVANCED (once patterns emerge — you've done something 3+ times)
   /coo-systemize →  turn the repeated work into a documented system
```

**Reads as a sentence:** *Set up the business → get your tools → get contracts ready → scope each project → deliver it → get paid → eventually systemize what repeats.*

## The Discovery Boundary — CRO vs. COO

Both roles have a "discovery" conversation, but they are different conversations with different purposes:

| | `/cro-discovery` (CRO) | `/coo-scope` (COO) |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | SELL — understand pain deeply enough to win the deal | SCOPE — define exactly what's in, out, and "done" |
| **Framework** | SPIN Selling (Rackham) | CLEAR Discovery + 5 Scoping Questions (Patty) |
| **Timing** | *Before* the yes | *After* the yes |
| **Output** | Buyer pain map → feeds the pitch | Signed SOW → feeds delivery |

Keeping them in separate roles prevents the classic mistake of trying to scope a project before you've won it — or selling during what should be a scoping call.

## Connection to Other C-Suite Workflows

- **CEO → COO:** The capabilities required (Playing to Win question 4 — "what must we be uniquely good at?") tell the COO what operational muscle to build. The OKRs from `/ceo-okrs` set the operational targets.
- **CRO → COO:** When `/cro-pitch` closes a deal, `/coo-scope` takes over (sell → scope handoff). Pricing decisions from `/cro-pricing` drive payment terms in `/coo-contracts` and `/coo-getpaid`.
- **COO → CRO:** A reliable delivery system + happy clients feed `/cro-grow` (case studies, referrals). Bad delivery kills the growth flywheel.
- **COO ↔ CMO:** Permission Marketing assets (newsletter, email list from `/cmo-audience`) need ops infrastructure to run — a convergence point.
- **COO → CPO/CTO:** For the apps/workflows tracks, delivery systems hand off to product delivery (CPO) and build/ship cycles (CTO).

## Triggers and Trigger Re-Entry

### When the FULL COO sequence runs

| Trigger | What just happened | Why the full sequence runs |
|---|---|---|
| **New startup** | Founder starting from zero | Need the whole machine: setup → stack → contracts → scope → deliver → getpaid |
| **Major pivot** | `/ceo-pivot` declared | Delivery model may change; contracts and scope templates need rework |

### When a SUBSET re-fires

| Trigger | What just happened | Commands that re-fire | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scope creep on a project** | Client keeps adding "small" requests | `/coo-scope` (tighten SOW) + `/coo-contracts` (add change-request clause) | The #1 AI project killer — fix the scoping + contract language |
| **A client paid late (or didn't)** | Overdue invoice | `/coo-getpaid` (tighten terms, deposit rule, follow-up cadence) | Cashflow discipline gap |
| **Delivery felt chaotic** | Scrambled to hit a milestone | `/coo-deliver` (add checklist) + `/coo-systemize` (find the bottleneck) | Process reliability gap |
| **Did the same task 3+ times** | Repeated manual work | `/coo-systemize` | E-Myth trigger: it's time to build a system |
| **Tax surprise** | Underestimated what was owed | `/coo-getpaid` (fix the 25-30% set-aside rhythm) | Financial hygiene gap |
| **Outgrew the starter tools** | Hit 3+ clients | `/coo-stack` (add CRM + accounting) | Stack-graduation moment |
| **About to hire / delegate** | First contractor or VA | `/coo-systemize` (document the playbook) + EOS reference | The handoff requires documented systems |
| **Quarterly review** | End of OKR cycle | `/coo-systemize` (bottleneck check) | Periodic operations health check |

### Convergence triggers

| Trigger | Commands across roles |
|---|---|
| **Go-to-market launch** | `/coo-setup` + `/coo-contracts` + `/coo-getpaid` + `/coo-stack` locked → Launch Readiness Checklist for `/launch` |
| **First client signed** | `/cro-pitch` closes → `/coo-scope` → `/coo-deliver` → `/coo-getpaid` (the full handoff chain) |
| **First hire** | `/coo-systemize` + CPO role-definition + CTO onboarding doc |
