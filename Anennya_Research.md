# Anennya Research: Phase 1 — The 13 Universal Things

*Research compiled April 23, 2026 — Sources: Reddit, LinkedIn, Medium, YouTube, and business resources*

---

## Overview

These are the 13 universal things everyone needs to figure out when starting an AI business — whether consulting, building workflows, or developing apps. They came up repeatedly across Reddit (r/AIautomation, r/Entrepreneur, r/EntrepreneurRideAlong), LinkedIn, Medium, and business guides.

---

## The 13 Universal Things

### 1. Idea Validation — Will anyone actually pay for this?
Most people skip this step and build for months toward zero clients. The signal to look for isn't "is this a good idea?" — it's "has someone already complained about this problem and tried to pay someone to fix it?"

**The core question:** Can you find 3 real signals that people want this *before* you build anything?

---

### 2. Niche & Ideal Client — Who, specifically, are you for?
"I help businesses use AI" is not a niche. The research is unanimous: generalists lose to specialists, especially when you're starting out. The tighter the niche, the easier everything else gets (pricing, messaging, finding clients).

**The core question:** What industry + what problem + what type of person?

---

### 3. Positioning & Messaging — What do you actually say?
67% of software company websites now mention AI — the word has become noise. Successful AI businesses lead with *outcomes*, not technology. "I help real estate agents reclaim 10 hours a week" outperforms "I build AI automation solutions."

**The core question:** What problem do you solve, in one sentence, without using the word "AI"?

---

### 4. Pricing — How much, how do you charge, and for what?
Three main models everyone wrestles with:
- **Hourly/day rate** — simple but caps your income
- **Project-based / flat fee** — requires scoping; risk of underestimating
- **Monthly retainer** — most stable; requires ongoing value delivery

**The core question:** What model fits your service, and how do you set a price that doesn't make you cringe?

---

### 5. Legal & Business Structure — Do you need an LLC?
Single-member LLC is the most common first step. Costs $100–$500 to set up + ~$150–$800/year. Creates a legal firewall between your personal finances and your business, and adds credibility when signing contracts.

**The core question:** Sole proprietor or LLC — and what do you actually need to do to register?

---

### 6. Contracts & Getting Paid — The financial plumbing
Invoicing, payment terms, what to do when someone doesn't pay, scope creep clauses, intellectual property ownership of AI outputs. This is the #1 thing new AI business owners report being caught off guard by.

**The core question:** How do you get paid reliably without a lawyer on retainer?

---

### 7. Building Credibility Before You Have Clients
The chicken-and-egg problem: you need case studies to get clients, but you need clients to get case studies. Most successful early-stage AI businesses solve this by offering one or two free or reduced-price engagements specifically in exchange for a detailed testimonial.

**The core question:** How do you build social proof when you're starting from zero?

---

### 8. Finding & Landing Your First Client
Your first client almost always comes from your existing network — not cold outreach, not ads. After that, every happy client is your best salesperson. Warm referrals convert at dramatically higher rates than any other channel.

**The core question:** Who in your existing network has the problem you solve, and how do you have that conversation?

---

### 9. The Discovery Process — Scoping before you propose
Most scope creep, undercharging, and unhappy clients trace back to a bad (or missing) discovery conversation. Before any proposal, you need a repeatable way to understand: What's the actual problem? What does success look like? What's out of scope?

**The core question:** What questions do you ask before you send a proposal?

---

### 10. Delivery Systems — How to stop reinventing the wheel
The biggest time drain for new AI business owners is recreating the same process for every client. Templates for onboarding, status updates, handoffs, and final delivery — built once, reused every time — are what separate a business from a series of one-off jobs.

**The core question:** What's your repeatable process from "yes, I'll work with you" to "here's your final deliverable"?

---

### 11. Tools Stack — What do you actually need?
The total monthly cost for a lean AI business is usually under $200. The danger zones: paying for tools you don't use, or over-engineering before you have clients. Most people need: Claude/ChatGPT, a scheduling tool, a simple contract tool, Stripe or PayPal, and one project management tool.

**The core question:** What's the minimum toolset to run your business professionally?

---

### 12. Promotion & Visibility — How to talk about your work
This is the #1 behavioral gap for most new business owners. They do great work and tell nobody. The most effective approach: share the *process*, not just the outcome. Behind-the-scenes posts consistently outperform polished case studies for building trust.

**The core question:** How do you talk about your work without feeling like you're bragging?

---

### 13. Growing Beyond the First Client — Referrals, repeat business, and scaling
Most AI businesses that survive past 6 months do so on repeat clients and referrals — not a constant stream of new leads. The infrastructure for this is: a simple follow-up system, a referral ask you're comfortable making, and case studies that do your selling for you.

**The core question:** How do you turn one client into three?
