# Anennya Research: Phase 1 — The 13 Universal Things + Frameworks

*Research compiled April 23, 2026 — Sources: Reddit, LinkedIn, Medium, YouTube, and business resources*

---

## Overview

These are the 13 universal things everyone needs to figure out when starting an AI business — whether consulting, building workflows, or developing apps. They came up repeatedly across Reddit (r/AIautomation, r/Entrepreneur, r/EntrepreneurRideAlong), LinkedIn, Medium, and business guides.

---

## The 13 Universal Things

### 1. Idea Validation — Will anyone actually pay for this?
Most people skip this step and build for months toward zero clients. The signal to look for isn't "is this a good idea?" — it's "has someone already complained about this problem and tried to pay someone to fix it?"

**The core question:** Can you find 3 real signals that people want this *before* you build anything?

---

### 2. Niche & Ideal Client — Who, specifically, are you for?
"I help businesses use AI" is not a niche. The research is unanimous: generalists lose to specialists, especially when you're starting out. The tighter the niche, the easier everything else gets (pricing, messaging, finding clients).

**The core question:** What industry + what problem + what type of person?

---

### 3. Positioning & Messaging — What do you actually say?
67% of software company websites now mention AI — the word has become noise. Successful AI businesses lead with *outcomes*, not technology. "I help real estate agents reclaim 10 hours a week" outperforms "I build AI automation solutions."

**The core question:** What problem do you solve, in one sentence, without using the word "AI"?

---

### 4. Pricing — How much, how do you charge, and for what?
Three main models everyone wrestles with:
- **Hourly/day rate** — simple but caps your income
- **Project-based / flat fee** — requires scoping; risk of underestimating
- **Monthly retainer** — most stable; requires ongoing value delivery

**The core question:** What model fits your service, and how do you set a price that doesn't make you cringe?

---

### 5. Legal & Business Structure — Do you need an LLC?
Single-member LLC is the most common first step. Costs $100–$500 to set up + ~$150–$800/year. Creates a legal firewall between your personal finances and your business, and adds credibility when signing contracts.

**The core question:** Sole proprietor or LLC — and what do you actually need to do to register?

---

### 6. Contracts & Getting Paid — The financial plumbing
Invoicing, payment terms, what to do when someone doesn't pay, scope creep clauses, intellectual property ownership of AI outputs. This is the #1 thing new AI business owners report being caught off guard by.

**The core question:** How do you get paid reliably without a lawyer on retainer?

---

### 7. Building Credibility Before You Have Clients
The chicken-and-egg problem: you need case studies to get clients, but you need clients to get case studies. Most successful early-stage AI businesses solve this by offering one or two free or reduced-price engagements specifically in exchange for a detailed testimonial.

**The core question:** How do you build social proof when you're starting from zero?

---

### 8. Finding & Landing Your First Client
Your first client almost always comes from your existing network — not cold outreach, not ads. After that, every happy client is your best salesperson. Warm referrals convert at dramatically higher rates than any other channel.

**The core question:** Who in your existing network has the problem you solve, and how do you have that conversation?

---

### 9. The Discovery Process — Scoping before you propose
Most scope creep, undercharging, and unhappy clients trace back to a bad (or missing) discovery conversation. Before any proposal, you need a repeatable way to understand: What's the actual problem? What does success look like? What's out of scope?

**The core question:** What questions do you ask before you send a proposal?

---

### 10. Delivery Systems — How to stop reinventing the wheel
The biggest time drain for new AI business owners is recreating the same process for every client. Templates for onboarding, status updates, handoffs, and final delivery — built once, reused every time — are what separate a business from a series of one-off jobs.

**The core question:** What's your repeatable process from "yes, I'll work with you" to "here's your final deliverable"?

---

### 11. Tools Stack — What do you actually need?
The total monthly cost for a lean AI business is usually under $200. The danger zones: paying for tools you don't use, or over-engineering before you have clients. Most people need: Claude/ChatGPT, a scheduling tool, a simple contract tool, Stripe or PayPal, and one project management tool.

**The core question:** What's the minimum toolset to run your business professionally?

---

### 12. Promotion & Visibility — How to talk about your work
This is the #1 behavioral gap for most new business owners. They do great work and tell nobody. The most effective approach: share the *process*, not just the outcome. Behind-the-scenes posts consistently outperform polished case studies for building trust.

**The core question:** How do you talk about your work without feeling like you're bragging?

---

### 13. Growing Beyond the First Client — Referrals, repeat business, and scaling
Most AI businesses that survive past 6 months do so on repeat clients and referrals — not a constant stream of new leads. The infrastructure for this is: a simple follow-up system, a referral ask you're comfortable making, and case studies that do your selling for you.

**The core question:** How do you turn one client into three?

## Part 2: Frameworks for Answering Each Question

For each of the 13 areas, these are the proven frameworks people actually use — not invented here, but battle-tested across consulting, startups, and solo businesses.

---

### 1. Idea Validation

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **The Mom Test** (Rob Fitzpatrick) | A method for asking customer questions that can't be answered with polite lies. You ask about their life and past behavior, not their opinions about your idea. | Stops you from hearing "that sounds great!" and mistaking it for validation |
| **Lean Startup Build-Measure-Learn** (Eric Ries) | Rapid loop: form a hypothesis → build the smallest possible test → measure real signal → learn and repeat | Prevents building something for months with no market feedback |
| **2-20-200 Framework** | 2 hours of desk research → 20 hours talking to potential customers → only 200 hours to build *after* strong signals | Time-boxes validation so you don't over-invest before proving demand |
| **Fake Door / Smoke Test** | Create a simple landing page or offer describing the thing *before* it exists. If people sign up or inquire, demand is real. | Fastest real-world signal without building anything |

---

### 2. Niche & Ideal Client

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Niche, Nail, Scale** | Pick one narrow niche → win consistently there → only expand once you've mastered it | Generalists lose to specialists; this gives you permission to go narrow first |
| **3-Part ICP Framework** | Who (firmographics) + When (the trigger that makes them need you now) + Why Now (the macro trend creating urgency) | Gives you a complete picture of your best client, not just demographics |
| **Jobs to Be Done** (Clayton Christensen) | What "job" is your client hiring you to do? What were they doing before you existed? | Reveals what clients actually value vs. what you think you're selling |

---

### 3. Positioning & Messaging

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Obviously Awesome** (April Dunford) | 5-part positioning: competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value those deliver → target who cares most → market frame of reference | The clearest framework for saying what you do and why it beats the alternative |
| **StoryBrand** (Donald Miller) | The client is the hero; you are the guide. Lead with their problem, not your credentials. | Flips the script from "here's what I do" to "here's what you'll achieve" |
| **"Unlike X, We Y" contrast messaging** | Lead with what makes you different by naming what you're *not*, then what you are | Works especially well in a saturated market where everyone says the same thing |

---

### 4. Pricing

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Value-Based Pricing** (Blair Enns / Jonathan Stark) | Price based on the client's ROI, not your hours. Ask: what is solving this problem *worth* to them? | Breaks the hours-for-dollars trap; aligns your incentives with client outcomes |
| **3-Tier Anchoring (Good / Better / Best)** | Offer three packages at different price points. Most clients land in the middle. | Makes the middle option feel reasonable; anchors client expectations |
| **Productized Service Model** | Fixed scope + fixed price + repeatable delivery = a product you happen to deliver as a service | Easier to sell, easier to deliver, easier to scale — no custom pricing every time |
| **50/50 Payment Rule** | 50% upfront before work starts, 50% upon delivery | Protects your time; filters out non-serious clients; standard in consulting |

---

### 5. Legal & Business Structure

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **SBA Business Structure Decision Tree** | Sole Proprietor → LLC → S-Corp, based on income level and liability needs | Gives a clear decision path without needing a lawyer for the basics |
| **Solo Legal Setup Checklist** | Form LLC → Get EIN (free from IRS) → Open separate business bank account → Get a contract template | Four steps that create the financial and legal firewall between you and your business |

---

### 6. Contracts & Getting Paid

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple SOW Template** (Statement of Work) | One-page document: what you'll do, what you won't do, timeline, payment terms, what client provides | Prevents scope creep and misaligned expectations before work starts |
| **Net 15 / Net 30 Payment Terms** | Invoice due within 15 or 30 days of delivery; include a late fee clause | Sets professional expectations; gives you a paper trail if payment is late |
| **IP & AI Output Clause** | Specifies who owns the AI-generated work product | Increasingly important and often missed entirely in early contracts |

---

### 7. Building Credibility Before You Have Clients

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **The Free Work Ladder** | One or two free/discounted engagements → in exchange for a detailed testimonial + permission to write a case study | Breaks the chicken-and-egg problem with social proof |
| **Content Credibility Stack** | Certifications + testimonials + a workshop/webinar + documented results, stacked over time | Each element reinforces the others; you don't need all of them to start |
| **Build in Public** | Share the process of building your business openly — what you're learning, what's working, what isn't | Turns your learning curve into visible expertise; community trusts the journey |

---

### 8. Finding & Landing Your First Client

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **The Referral Map** | Write down 15–20 names from memory → assign comfort level → warm them up on LinkedIn → ask for intros | Your first client is almost always in your existing network; this makes that systematic |
| **Hormozi First 5 Clients** (Alex Hormozi) | Export your contacts → pick the platform you have most → send personalized messages → ask for referrals → make an easy offer | Simple, non-salesy framework that leverages existing trust |
| **Show, Don't Tell Demo** | Instead of describing what you do, build a small demo of your service applied to *their* business and show it | Turns a pitch into a proof; dramatically increases conversion |

---

### 9. The Discovery Process

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **CLEAR Discovery Framework** | Capture the goal → Learn their current approach → Examine emotions → Acknowledge and confirm → Reveal additional context | Structured discovery that captures both practical and emotional context |
| **5 Core Scoping Questions** | What problem are you solving? What does success look like? What's your deadline? What have you already tried? What's out of scope? | If you can answer these five, you can write any proposal or SOW |
| **Co-Created Roadmap** | During the kickoff call, build the project plan *with* the client, not for them | Eliminates scope creep because the client defined the scope themselves |

---

### 10. Delivery Systems

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Client Journey Map** | Pre-onboarding → Kickoff → Milestone check-ins → Final delivery → Offboarding/handoff | Makes every engagement feel professional and predictable from the client's perspective |
| **Template Library** | Reusable docs for proposals, SOWs, status updates, final delivery, and offboarding | Build once, reuse forever — stops you from recreating the wheel on every project |
| **Productized Delivery Playbook** | Step-by-step internal doc for how you deliver your signature service | Turns a custom service into a repeatable product; foundation for eventually delegating |

---

### 11. Tools Stack

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimum Viable Stack** | Claude/ChatGPT + Calendly + HelloSign or Docusign + Stripe + Notion or Trello | Under $150/month; covers every operational need for a solo AI business |
| **Tool Audit Rule** | If you haven't used a tool in 30 days, cancel it | Prevents subscription creep that quietly drains cash flow |

---

### 12. Promotion & Visibility

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **Build in Public** | Share the process, not just the outcome — the messy middle, the decisions, the lessons | Most trusted form of content marketing; creates community, not just followers |
| **1-to-5 Repurposing** | One piece of real work → 5 content pieces: a post, a short video, a quote, a thread, a newsletter snippet | Removes the "I don't have time to create content" excuse |
| **StoryBrand for Social** | Every post: here's a problem your audience has → here's what I learned → here's what you can do | Keeps content useful and audience-focused, not self-promotional |
| **$1.80 Strategy** (Gary Vee) | Comment meaningfully on 9 posts per day in your niche, leaving your genuine "2 cents" | Builds visibility and relationship capital before you ask for anything |

---

### 13. Growing Beyond the First Client

| Framework | What it is | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| **The Direct Referral Ask** | At project close: "Who else do you know who has this problem? Would you be comfortable introducing me?" | Most people won't refer without being asked directly; this normalizes the ask |
| **Case Study Template** | Problem → What we did → Result (quantified if possible) → Client quote | The most powerful sales tool for service businesses; does your selling for you |
| **Upsell Ladder** | Project → Retainer → Expanded scope | Natural progression that grows revenue without finding new clients |
