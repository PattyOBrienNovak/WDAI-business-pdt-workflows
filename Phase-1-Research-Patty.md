# Phase 1 Research: The 10–15 Things Everyone Needs to Figure Out When Starting an AI Business

*Researched April 14, 2026 — Patty O'Brien Novak & Anennya (parallel research tracks)*
*Sources: Reddit communities, LinkedIn, Medium, YouTube, GitHub, consulting industry guides*

---

## How to Read This

These are the 13 things that come up again and again across every source — regardless of whether someone is starting an AI consulting practice, building automation workflows for clients, or developing AI-powered apps. They're ordered the way most people actually encounter them (messy reality, not ideal sequence).

Each one includes: **what it is**, **why it's a real blocker**, and **what the research says actually works**.

---

## 1. Validate Before You Build

**What it is:** Confirming people will actually pay for your idea before you spend weeks building it.

**Why it's a blocker:** Most people skip this and build something nobody wants to pay for, or they pick a niche based on what sounds interesting rather than where real pain and budget exist.

**What actually works:**
- Apply the "3 filters" test: Does the problem happen at least weekly? Does it cost the business $50K+ per year? Is there a budget owner who can approve spending within 30 days?
- Aim for 10 real conversations with potential clients before building anything
- "Validate with currency" — are they willing to pay, even a small amount, before it's built?
- Start with a low-risk offer ($1,500–$2,500 audit or diagnostic) to test demand and build your first case study simultaneously

**The trap:** Defining your audience too broadly because you're afraid of missing out. The smaller and more specific your initial target, the faster everything works — messaging, outreach, conversion.

---

## 2. Pick a Niche (and Resist Going Wide)

**What it is:** Choosing ONE industry, problem, and type of client to serve — at least to start.

**Why it's a blocker:** The #1 mistake AI business owners make is trying to be a generalist ("We do AI for everyone"). Specialized consultants command 30–40% fee premiums over generalists. Clients increasingly search for domain-specific expertise, not general AI knowledge.

**What actually works:**
- Your niche isn't what you do — it's who you serve and what specific problem you solve for them
- Pick based on: where you have existing knowledge or experience + where real pain and budget exists
- You don't need to be an industry expert — you just need to know more about AI than they do
- Start with a "beachhead" market where you can be 10x better than alternatives, then expand
- The irony: trying to appeal to everyone means you appeal to no one

**Examples of viable niches:** AI automation for real estate agencies, AI content workflows for e-commerce brands, AI customer service for dental practices, AI operations tools for HR consultants.

---

## 3. Define Your Ideal Client Profile (ICP)

**What it is:** A specific description of exactly who your best client is — industry, company size, problems they have, how they make buying decisions.

**Why it's a blocker:** Without a clear ICP, your outreach feels generic, your messaging doesn't land, and you waste time talking to people who will never buy.

**What actually works:**
- Include: industry, company size, growth stage, specific pain points, budget authority, and what "expensive problem" they have that you solve
- Validate by interviewing at least 10 real potential clients — don't build your ICP from assumptions
- Treat it as an iterative loop — refine based on actual sales calls and closed deals
- Most common mistake: building an ICP from guesses, not conversations

---

## 4. Set Up Your Business Structure

**What it is:** Deciding whether to operate as a sole proprietor or LLC, getting your EIN, opening a business bank account, and handling basic tax setup.

**Why it's a blocker:** Many enterprise clients won't engage sole proprietors — their procurement departments require a formal business entity. Also, without a business bank account, you can't separate personal and business finances, which creates tax headaches and looks unprofessional.

**What actually works:**
- LLC is the right move for most AI consultants: creates a legal firewall between personal and business finances, signals legitimacy to clients, costs $100–$500 to form + $150–$800/year in state fees
- Get an EIN (free, takes 10 minutes at IRS.gov) — required for business bank accounts and tax filing
- Open a dedicated business bank account immediately — this is the single most important operational habit
- You can start invoicing as a sole proprietor under your own name while you set up the LLC

## 5. Price Your Services (and Don't Underprice)

**What it is:** Deciding how you charge — hourly, project-based, retainer, or productized — and setting rates that reflect the value you deliver.

**Why it's a blocker:** Underpricing is the most common mistake. Half of potential clients report budgets under $2,000 — those aren't your clients. Pricing too low also signals low confidence and attracts difficult clients.

**What actually works — current rates (2025–2026):**
- Hourly: $100–$150 (junior) → $150–$300 (mid) → $300–$500+ (expert)
- Day rates: $600–$1,200/day
- Project-based: $10K–$40K (small/pilot) → $40K–$150K (medium, multi-month)
- Retainers: $2K–$5K/month (5–10 hrs) → $5K–$15K/month (10–25 hrs)
- Productized services: most scalable; 44% of consultants now offer a productized package

**The trend:** Value-based pricing (charging 10–40% of cost savings or revenue increase you produce) is growing fast. Tie your price to their outcome, not your time.

**Quick win:** Offer a fixed-price audit ($1,500–$2,500) as your entry offer — lower risk for the client, easier to sell, and builds your case study portfolio.

## 6. Get Your First Clients

**What it is:** Landing the first 2–3 paying clients — the hardest milestone in any consulting business.

**Why it's a blocker:** Most people either wait for clients to come to them (they don't) or go straight to cold outreach (premature). The path that actually works starts warmer.

**What actually works:**
1. **Start warm:** Former coworkers, managers, classmates, business contacts — tell them what you're doing and ask if they know anyone who could use your help
2. **LinkedIn:** Search for people matching your ICP, connect with a personalized note (not a pitch), and add value before asking for anything
3. **Cold email:** Works, but requires volume (100+ per week) and good deliverability setup — better for phase 2
4. **Low-risk first offer:** Lead with an audit or diagnostic, not a full engagement — easier "yes" for them, proof of value for you
5. **Pilot discount:** Offer your first engagement at a reduced rate in exchange for a testimonial and the right to use them as a case study

**What doesn't work:** Waiting for referrals before you have any clients to give them. You need to create the first 2–3 manually.

## 7. Build Your Delivery System (Before You Need It)

**What it is:** Having a clear, repeatable process for how you do your work — from client onboarding through final delivery — so you don't reinvent it for every client.

**Why it's a blocker:** Over 50% of AI consulting projects fail due to scope creep and unclear priorities (McKinsey 2025). The failure pattern: consultant delivers a proof of concept, client is excited, then nothing gets implemented and the relationship falls apart.

**What actually works:**
- Create a client onboarding checklist before you need it
- Define scope in writing before any work starts — what's included, what's not, what done looks like
- Use a simple project management tool (ClickUp, Notion, Asana) from day one
- Build a Statement of Work template you can customize — not a contract you write from scratch each time
- Plan for change requests: add a "change request" clause to every contract so scope creep has a process

**The delivery gap:** Many AI consultants are good at strategy but bad at seeing implementation through. Clients pay for outcomes, not decks.

## 8. Handle the Legal and Money Basics

**What it is:** Contracts, invoicing, getting paid on time, basic tax hygiene.

**Why it's a blocker:** Many first-time consultants either skip contracts (and get burned) or overthink them (and delay getting started). And not knowing how to handle taxes as a self-employed person is a common, expensive surprise.

**What actually works:**
- **Contracts:** Use a simple service agreement template — cover scope, payment terms, IP ownership, confidentiality, and a change request process. Free templates exist; you don't need a lawyer for straightforward engagements
- **Invoicing:** Net 15 or Net 30 payment terms are standard. Require a 25–50% deposit upfront on project work. Use Wave (free), FreshBooks, or QuickBooks
- **Getting paid:** Follow up on overdue invoices on day 1 after the due date — being timely about this is a skill, not rudeness
- **Taxes:** Set aside 25–30% of every payment for taxes. Pay quarterly estimated taxes. Keep business and personal expenses strictly separate
- **What many enterprise clients require:** A W-9, a vendor onboarding form, and sometimes proof of business insurance

## 9. Set Up Your Operations Stack

**What it is:** The tools you use to run your business day-to-day — not to serve clients, but to operate your business.

**Why it's a blocker:** Using the wrong tools (or no tools) creates invisible friction — missed follow-ups, lost invoices, scattered client files, no visibility into what's happening.

**Recommended starter stack:**
| Function | Tool | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| CRM / pipeline | HubSpot (free tier) or Notion | Free |
| Project management | ClickUp or Asana | Free tier |
| Invoicing | Wave or FreshBooks | Free/~$15/mo |
| Contracts | DocuSign or HelloSign | ~$15/mo |
| Scheduling | Calendly | Free tier |
| Accounting | QuickBooks Online | ~$30/mo |
| Communication | Slack or email | — |

**The integration principle:** Tools that connect to each other multiply their value. At minimum, connect your calendar → scheduling tool → CRM so you never lose track of a conversation.

## 10. Position Yourself and Define Your Offer

**What it is:** Being clear on what you offer, who it's for, why you and not someone else, and how to describe it in one sentence a non-technical person understands.

**Why it's a blocker:** Most AI business owners can describe what they do technically but can't explain the business outcome in plain language. Clients buy outcomes, not technology.

**What actually works:**
- One-sentence offer formula: *"I help [specific type of client] [achieve specific outcome] without [the thing they're afraid of or trying to avoid]."*
- Your differentiation doesn't have to be your technology — it can be your industry focus, your process, your speed, your teaching style, or your lived experience
- Test your positioning by saying it out loud to someone in your target market. If they don't immediately say "oh, I need that" or "I know someone who does," rewrite it

## 11. Promote Yourself Consistently (Not Occasionally)

**What it is:** Building visibility in your market through content, community, and outreach — consistently, not just when you need clients.

**Why it's a blocker:** Most people think promotion means self-aggrandizement. The research says the opposite: authenticity and teaching outperform polished brand content. The problem isn't that people don't want to promote themselves — it's that they only do it when business is slow, by which point it's too late.

**What actually works in 2025–2026:**
- **Building in public:** Showing your process, including the messy parts, builds more trust than polished case studies. Community over reach.
- **80/20 content approach:** Use AI to produce volume (80% of posts, newsletters, short content), spend your energy on the 20% that shows real expertise — original observations, hard-won lessons, data you gathered yourself
- **One platform, consistently:** Pick LinkedIn, Twitter/X, or a newsletter. Show up 3–5x per week for 90 days before evaluating results
- **Turn every client win into content:** A project → a case study → a LinkedIn post → a newsletter → a framework you teach. One thing, five pieces
- **Promotion at every stage:** Don't wait until you have a polished offering. "Here's what I'm discovering about [niche]" is promotable content even when you're just getting started

## 12. Manage Client Expectations (Especially Around AI)

**What it is:** Being honest with clients about what AI can and can't do, setting realistic timelines, and educating as you go.

**Why it's a blocker:** AI has been overpromised for years. Only ~30% of AI consulting projects are seen as completely successful by clients. The gap is almost always expectations, not capability.

**What actually works:**
- Underpromise on timelines, overpromise on communication frequency
- Start with a pilot or proof of concept on a small, measurable problem before full engagement
- Educate clients on how AI actually works — clients who understand it are easier to work with
- Define success metrics before starting: "We will know this worked when X changes by Y amount"
- Most failed projects lack three things: clear goals, clean data, and a plan for integrating into daily workflows — address all three upfront

---

## 13. Plan to Grow (Case Studies, Referrals, Upsells)

**What it is:** Turning your first clients into the engine for your next clients — through referrals, testimonials, case studies, and repeat business.

**Why it's a blocker:** Growth is not automatic. Most consultants stay stuck at 2–3 clients because they don't build the referral and case study systems that would let them grow.

**What actually works:**
- Ask for a referral at the moment of highest client satisfaction (when you deliver something that delights them — not at the end of the engagement when they're just grateful it's over)
- Write a case study for every completed project: problem → approach → result. Three sentences minimum
- Design natural upsell paths: if you did a one-time audit, what's the logical next engagement? Have it ready before the first one ends
- Community visibility (like building in public with WDAI) generates the kind of organic referrals that cold outreach never produces

## What the Research Says About Track-Specific Differences

### Consulting track
- First client almost always comes from your existing network
- Scope creep is the #1 delivery risk — manage contracts carefully
- Specialization commands 30–40% higher fees than generalism

### Workflows / Automation track
- Cold outreach (using the automation tools you sell) is the most common client acquisition channel
- Demo videos showing real automation workflows convert better than any other marketing
- Pricing resistance is high — ROI calculators and graduated service tiers overcome it
- Target businesses already experiencing growth and struggling to scale — they're most receptive

### Apps track
- Validation is even more important — the build cost is higher
- A non-technical founder needs a technical co-founder or contractor before anything else
- MVP scoping discipline is critical — most app projects fail from over-scoping

## The Overlooked Things (What Most Guides Skip)

1. **Getting paid on time is a skill.** Invoice the moment a milestone is hit. Follow up on day 1 after a due date. This feels uncomfortable but is normal and expected in professional services.

2. **The first 6–12 months will consume more internal time than client time.** Building your templates, onboarding processes, pricing, and systems takes longer than expected. Budget for it.

3. **Generative AI is rapidly changing what "junior AI consultant" means.** ~25% of consulting skills will become obsolete by mid-2026. The response is continuous learning, not waiting for things to stabilize.

4. **Community is a competitive advantage.** In a commoditizing market, people buy from people they know and trust. WDAI is itself a moat — lean into it.
